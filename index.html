Author

Emily Jane Brontë an English novelist and poet, was born in July, 1818 in Thornton, Yorkshire, England. Emily was part of a talented literary family. Her sisters, Charlotte and Anne, also became well-known writers.

Emily’s formal education began when she attended the Clergy Daughters’ School at Cowan Bridge, but she was later removed from the school along with her sisters due to poor conditions and the outbreak of tuberculosis. After leaving the school, Emily was primarily educated at home by her father.

Emily Brontë’s literary career is primarily centered around “Wuthering Heights” which was published in 1847 under the pseudonym Ellis Bell.

The novel is known for its dark, passionate characters and its exploration of themes such as love, revenge, and the supernatural. Despite initial mixed reviews, “Wuthering Heights” gained popularity over time and is now considered one of the greatest novels in the English language.

Emily Brontë led a relatively secluded life, spending much of her time with her family at the parsonage in Haworth, Yorkshire. She was known for her love of nature and her solitary nature. Her experiences growing up in the rugged Yorkshire moors greatly influenced her writing, providing the atmospheric backdrop for her novels.

Emily Brontë’s literary career was cut short by illness. She died of tuberculosis in December 1848 at the age of 30, just one year after the publication of “Wuthering Heights.”

Introduction

Introduction

“Wuthering Heights” is a timeless classic of English literature, written by Emily Brontë and published in 1847. Set against the atmospheric backdrop of the Yorkshire moors, Brontë’s only novel is tackled the haunting tale of love, revenge, and the destructive power of passion.

At its heart, “Wuthering Heights” explores the tumultuous relationships between the inhabitants of two neighboring households: the Earnshaws of Wuthering Heights and the Lintons of Thrushcross Grange. Through the eyes of the narrator, Mr. Lockwood, and later through the recollections of the housekeeper, Nelly Dean, the reader is drawn into a complex web of family secrets, forbidden love, and vengeful obsession.

Central to the novel is the passionate and destructive love between Catherine Earnshaw and Heathcliff, an orphaned boy taken in by the Earnshaw family. Their bond defies social conventions and transcends mere affection, but it is also marked by jealousy, manipulation, and cruelty.

As the story unfolds, Brontë delves deep into the dark recesses of the human psyche, exploring themes of class conflict, revenge, and the cyclical nature of violence.

The novel’s setting plays a crucial role in shaping its mood and atmosphere. The desolate Yorkshire moors mirror the wild and untamed emotions of the characters, while the isolated and windswept landscape serves as a metaphor for their turbulent inner lives.

From the imposing presence of the titular Wuthering Heights to the more refined surroundings of Thrushcross Grange, the contrasting environments reflect the stark divisions within society and the characters themselves.

A Happy Ending

After three long years abroad, I returned to England, landing in London one autumn evening. I immediately went to see Traddles, who was now working as a lawyer. He had finally married his pretty Sophy. I found him in his chambers and told the clerk that I wished to see him. “Goodness!” cried Traddles, “It’s Copperfield!” and rushed into my arms. “How wonderful to see you! You have grown so famous.
